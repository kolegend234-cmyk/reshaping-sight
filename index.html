01 The Eye, First

Before we fix it, we have to understand it.

Vision is a two-lens problem. Light enters through the cornea, the clear dome at the front of the eye, which does about two-thirds of the eye's focusing work. It then passes through the lens, a flexible disc that fine-tunes the focus, and strikes the retina, a light-sensitive sheet at the back of the eye. Every laser eye surgery you're about to see modifies one of these three parts.

The Problem: Myopia

In a nearsighted eye, the cornea is too curved, or the eye is too long, so light converges to a focus in front of the retina instead of on it. Distant objects blur.

Glasses and contacts fix this by adding a correcting lens in front of the eye. LASIK fixes it permanently by reshaping the cornea itself, flattening its center so rays converge further back, exactly on the retina.

The Procedure — tap through the steps

Create the Flap

A suction ring holds the eye still. A femtosecond laser fires pulses so short, one-quadrillionth of a second each, that they create tiny plasma bubbles beneath the surface of the cornea. Those bubbles line up to form a precise horizontal cut, leaving a thin circular flap of tissue still attached at one edge like a hinge. No blade touches the eye.

Why Two Different Lasers

Femtosecond & Excimer

The femtosecond laser uses near-infrared pulses to make clean cuts inside the cornea without damaging the surface. The excimer laser uses ultraviolet pulses that break molecular bonds in the stroma, removing tissue without generating heat, so the surrounding cornea stays intact.

How the Cornea Changes Focus

Flattening the Curve

A more curved cornea bends light more strongly. To correct myopia, the excimer laser removes more tissue from the center of the stroma than the edges. This flattens the overall curvature, weakens the cornea's focusing power, and moves the focal point back onto the retina.

Why the Flap Heals Without Stitches

Epithelial Regeneration

The cornea's outer layer, the epithelium, regenerates completely every 7–14 days. When the flap is replaced, it adheres to the eye through natural suction and begins re-bonding within hours. No sutures are needed.

The Trade-Off

Thinner Cornea, Weaker Mechanics

LASIK permanently thins the cornea, usually by 60–120 micrometers. This is generally safe, but patients with already-thin corneas or irregularly shaped corneas (like keratoconus) are poor candidates. Long-term flap integrity under trauma also remains a mild concern.

03 Surgery Two / SMILE

Extract a lenticule.
No flap needed.

SMILE (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction) is the newest laser refractive surgery. Instead of cutting a flap, it carves a thin disc of tissue inside the cornea and pulls it out through a 2–4 mm slit.

The Problem: Myopia & astigmatism

SMILE treats the same refractive errors as LASIK (nearsightedness and astigmatism), but patients with thinner corneas, dry eyes, or active lifestyles (contact sports, military) often prefer it. Without a flap, there's nothing to dislodge during trauma, and fewer corneal nerves are severed, so post-surgery dry eye is much less common.

The goal is the same: reshape the cornea to bend incoming light onto the retina. The method is very different.

The Procedure — tap through the steps

Carve the Lenticule

The femtosecond laser fires pulses at two different depths inside the stroma, carving out a thin lens-shaped disc of tissue called the lenticule. The size and shape of this disc is calculated from the patient's prescription: a thicker lenticule for stronger corrections, a thinner one for milder myopia.

Why No Flap

Structural Integrity Preserved

LASIK severs the strong collagen fibers of the upper cornea to create its flap. SMILE leaves those fibers almost entirely intact, cutting only a small 2–4 mm opening. The cornea retains more of its original mechanical strength.

One Laser, One Machine

Only Femtosecond Needed

LASIK requires handing the patient between two lasers: femtosecond for the flap, excimer for reshaping. SMILE does both the shaping (by defining the lenticule) and the access cut with a single femtosecond laser, reducing procedure complexity.

The Problem: Cataracts

The eye's natural lens is made mostly of proteins called crystallins, arranged so precisely that they're perfectly transparent. Over decades, UV exposure, oxidative stress, and age cause these proteins to misfold and clump. The lens yellows, then clouds. Incoming light is scattered rather than focused, so vision becomes hazy, colors dull, glare increases.

Unlike myopia, you can't fix this with reshaping. The broken lens has to come out.

The Procedure — tap through the steps

Corneal Incision

The femtosecond laser creates a tiny self-sealing incision at the edge of the cornea, usually 2–3 mm wide. This is the access point through which every later tool will enter. In traditional cataract surgery, this cut is made with a blade; the laser produces a more precise, architecturally uniform incision.

Why Fragment Before Removing

The Lens Is Too Big

The natural lens is roughly 9 mm across, but the incision is only 2–3 mm. It can't come out whole. The laser softens and fragments the lens into pieces small enough to be suctioned out through the phaco probe.

What Replaces the Lens

The IOL

An intraocular lens is a flexible acrylic or silicone disc that can be rolled up, inserted through the small incision, and unfolded into place inside the now-empty lens capsule. Some IOLs correct for myopia or astigmatism at the same time, giving better post-surgery vision than the patient had at any point in their life.

Ultrasound, Not Just Laser

Phacoemulsification

FLACS is laser-assisted, not fully laser. The actual removal of lens fragments is done by the phaco probe: a titanium tip vibrating ~40,000 times per second that emulsifies (liquefies) the tissue while simultaneously suctioning it out.

What the Laser Adds

Precision & Repeatability

Every step the laser performs (corneal incision, capsulotomy, lens fragmentation) can also be done by hand. But the laser's cuts are more circular, more predictable, and can be planned in advance using OCT imaging of the patient's specific eye.

05 Side by Side

Same goal.
Different engineering.

All three procedures solve the same underlying physics problem: the eye's optical system is failing to bring light to a focused point on the retina. They just take completely different paths to the fix.
